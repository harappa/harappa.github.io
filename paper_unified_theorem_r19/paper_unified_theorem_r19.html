1 Introduction

Metastability—the phenomenon whereby a stochastic system remains trapped near a local equilibrium for an exponentially long time before transitioning to a distant equilibrium—is a central theme in mathematical statistical mechanics. In continuous state spaces, the theory is well developed: the Kramers formula for diffusions (Kramers, 1940), the potential-theoretic approach of Bovier et al. (2001, 2002) (hereafter BEGK), and the monograph of Bovier and den Hollander (2015) provide a comprehensive framework for computing transition times via capacities and Dirichlet forms. In discrete state spaces, particularly on the high-dimensional hypercube {0, 1}d, the picture is far less complete.

The closest antecedent in the discrete setting is the work of Ding et al. (2009) on the mixing time of Glauber dynamics for the mean-field Ising model (Curie–Weiss model). They established a sharp cutoff window of width O() for the total-variation mixing time. More recently, Kim and Seo (2025) extended BEGK-type capacity estimates to certain discrete spin systems. On the combinatorial side, the static structure of NK fitness landscapes has been studied by Kauffman and Levin (1987), Durrett and Limic (2003), and Limic and Pemantle (2004), but all of these results concern the landscape geometry (number of local optima, fitness correlations) rather than the dynamics of Markov chains on the landscape. To our knowledge, no prior work establishes rigorous metastability results—capacity asymptotics, hitting-time formulas, or threshold scaling—for Metropolis dynamics on NK fitness landscapes.

The present paper fills this gap by proving quenched, K-independent metastable asymptotics for the NK Metropolis chain on {0, 1}d. Our results identify a robust K-independent asymptotic mechanism—which we refer to as product-space expander-type metastability—characterized by three features: (i) the window width scales as O((log d)∕d) (Regime I) or O((log T + log d)∕) (Regime II), qualitatively different from the O() window of the mean-field Ising model; (ii) the rate function Ψ is K-independent—microscopic epistasis does not affect the macroscopic barrier; (iii) the barrier scales linearly in d near the critical point. Whether this mechanism extends to other product-space Markov chains and constitutes a universality class in the sense of statistical mechanics remains an open question. These results are established for two qualitatively different landscape geometries (interior saddle vs. boundary kink), under intensive normalization (κ = κ0∕d), with full O(log d) precision for K = O(1). For growing K, the error degrades gracefully: see Remark 2.3.

Logical dependence on the companion paper. The K = 0 base case—where the chain projects onto a birth-death chain on {0,…,d}—is treated in the companion paper (Nashida, 2026a), which provides the explicit computation engine for Ψ, λc, and Ψ′(λc). The present paper imports the following results from (Nashida, 2026a): (a) the existence and properties of m+(λ) and msad(λ) in both regimes (used in Assumption 2.5); (b) the K = 0 Regime II asymptotics including the boundary Laplace lemma and the quadratic expansion Ψ(λ) = c2λ2 + O(λ3) (Lemmas 5.1, 5.4, 5.9 of Nashida 2026a); (c) the small-λ uniformity of error bounds in Regime II (Lemma 5.12 of Nashida 2026a). All other results in the present paper are self-contained. The companion paper does not use any result from the present one; there is no circular dependence.

The present paper establishes K-independence via Dirichlet form comparison (capacity stability under NK perturbation) and concentration inequalities (McDiarmid’s inequality for the quenched barrier), then invokes the K = 0 formulas to obtain universal results. Numerical predictions of the theory are verified in the simulation study (Nashida, 2026c).

Three structural properties suffice for the entire argument: reversibility, ±1-step structure, and uniform ellipticity. In Regime I, uniformity in λ ∈ [λc,λc + δ0] is guaranteed by Lemma 4.8 ; in Regime II, uniformity on compact subsets of (0,∞) follows from strict concavity (A3′) and the fixed kink (A4′), with the extension to λ → 0+ supplied by Lemma 5.12 of Nashida (2026a).

11.2 Comparison with Curie–Weiss metastability

The closest antecedent to our results is the work of Ding et al. (2009) on Glauber dynamics for the mean-field Ising (Curie–Weiss) model on {0, 1}n. Both settings share the key structural feature of a Hamming-weight projection yielding a birth-death chain with a double-well free energy. The differences, however, are fundamental.

Window width. Ding et al. (2009) established a mixing-time cutoff at βc = 1 with window width O() in the inverse-temperature parameter. In our framework, the Regime I metastable window is O((log d)∕d)—the same order in d when log T = Θ(log d)—while the Regime II window is O((log T + log d)∕). The qualitative difference arises because our rate function Ψ vanishes linearly in Regime I but quadratically in Regime II, whereas the Curie–Weiss free-energy barrier vanishes linearly at the critical inverse temperature.

Observable. Our results concern the hitting time between basins (a metastability observable), not the mixing time (a global equilibration observable). These are related but distinct: hitting time controls the metastable lifetime, while mixing time describes convergence to full equilibrium.

Landscape structure. The Curie–Weiss model has a fixed quadratic Hamiltonian, while our framework allows general basin functions B satisfying (A1)–(A5) or (A1′)–(A4′), and proves that NK epistatic perturbations do not affect the leading asymptotics. More recently, Kim and Seo (2025) extended BEGK-type capacity estimates to certain discrete spin systems; our results complement this direction by providing a complete metastability theory for the NK family, including quenched K-independence and explicit threshold formulas.

11.3 The product-space expander mechanism

The Introduction identified three features characterizing the present metastable mechanism, which we termed product-space expander-type metastability: (i) the window width scales as O((log d)∕d) or O((log T + log d)∕), qualitatively different from Curie–Weiss; (ii) the rate function Ψ is K-independent—microscopic epistasis does not affect the macroscopic barrier; (iii) the barrier scales linearly in d near the critical point.

All three features are established by the present work. Features (i) and (ii) hold in both regimes (Theorems 2.10–2.14 ); feature (iii) is specific to Regime I, where the critical point λc > 0 produces a barrier dΨ that is linear in d for λ near λc. (In Regime II, the barrier onset is quadratic: dΨ(λ) ∼ c2dλ2, which is linear in d at fixed λ but vanishes at λ → 0+; the relevant threshold scaling β∗ ∼ already absorbs this distinction.) Features (i) and (iii) distinguish the mechanism from Curie–Weiss metastability (Section 11.2); feature (ii) is the main novelty, showing that the high-dimensional product structure of {0, 1}d renders subextensive epistatic perturbations invisible at the exponential barrier scale.

Whether this mechanism extends to other product-space Markov chains—for example, q-ary hypercubes {1,…,q}d with external fitness landscapes, or Glauber dynamics on random regular graphs with planted community structure—remains an open question. Establishing (or refuting) such extensions would determine whether the present results constitute a universality class in the sense of statistical mechanics.

11.4 Connection to simulation results

The companion simulation study (Nashida, 2026c) discovered the three-regime structure (diffusive, punctuated, frozen) empirically and observed K-independence of regime boundaries for K = 0–16 at d = 10–150. The present paper provides rigorous confirmation: Theorem 2.10 proves K-independence at the exponential scale, and Corollary 2.15 yields the explicit threshold β∗ ∼∕s (asymptotic exponent 1∕2). The simulation fitted exponent 0.34 ± 0.03 over d = 10–300 is consistent with 1∕2 subject to finite-size corrections: the higher-order terms in the rate function Ψ(λ) = c2λ2 + O(λ3) (Theorem 2.12) produce a systematic downward bias at moderate d.

The simulation also observed that the punctuated regime vanishes at d ≈ 20. This is a finite-size crossover effect. The empirically fitted lower boundary βlow∗ ≈ 5.7 ln d − 6.1 (Nashida, 2026c, Eq. 4) grows logarithmically, while the theoretically predicted upper boundary βhigh∗ = ∕s (Corollary 2.15) grows as d1∕2; the prefactor mismatch causes the lower boundary to exceed the upper boundary at moderate d, closing the punctuated band. In the d →∞ limit, d1∕2 eventually dominates ln d, but the biologically relevant regime is the finite-size crossover near d ≈ 20.

11.5 Open problems

(a)
Sharp Eyring–Kramers prefactor. The present results give log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ + O(log d). The Eyring–Kramers formula predicts log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ + log d + C + o(1), where the constant involves Φλ′′(m+), Φλ′′(mbar), and the local transition rates. Establishing this for the discrete hypercube chain would require sharper Laplace asymptotics with explicit subleading terms.
(b)
Growing K. For 𝒦 = sκ0(K + 1) = ω(log d), the error becomes O(𝒦) (Propositions 9.8, 9.10 ). Whether O(log d) holds for K = ω(1) is open. The bottleneck is the hitting-time transfer (Propositions 9.8, 9.10), where the within-layer π-ratio bound contributes O(𝒦).
(c)
Extensive regime (κ = O(1)). The within-layer π-ratio can be eO(d), making the superharmonic comparison inapplicable. The quenched barrier fluctuation is Θ(sκ) (Appendix C), producing an O(1) per-site shift that requires qualitatively different tools.
(d)
Concentration of τRd. The present results control 𝔼[τRd] but not the fluctuations of τRd itself. Proving τRd∕𝔼[τRd] → 1 in probability would sharpen the diffusive-to-frozen transition and strengthen the connection to the simulation-observed sharp phase boundaries.
(e)
Product-space universality. Extending the K-independence mechanism to q-ary product spaces {1,…,q}d, non-symmetric basin functions, or non-nearest-neighbor Metropolis proposals would clarify the scope of the product-space expander mechanism identified here.
