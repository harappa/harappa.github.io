Metastability on the hypercube I:
rate functions and sharp thresholds for Metropolis dynamics

Hiroyuki Nashida
Independent Researcher, Yokohama, Japan

Abstract

We study the metastability of the single-bit Metropolis chain on the binary hypercube {0,1}d under a symmetric fitness function that depends only on the Hamming weight. Since the chain projects exactly onto a birth-death chain on {0,…,d}, we exploit one-dimensional capacity theory (series resistance on a path graph) and discrete Laplace asymptotics to obtain exact exponential-scale results.

We prove three main theorems. First, equilibrium delocalization: whenever the selection strength β = o(d), the stationary mass in the two endpoint basins vanishes. Second, for smooth basin functions admitting a supercritical double-well free energy (Regime I), the mean transition time between basins satisfies log 𝔼μL d[τRd] = dΨ(λ)+ O(log d) with an explicit rate function Ψ vanishing at a critical threshold λc > 0, and the freezing threshold satisfies λfr = λc + log T∕(dΨ′(λc)) + O((log T)2∕d2 + (log d)∕d). Third, for piecewise-smooth basin functions with a kink at m = 1∕2 (Regime II), we establish an analogous rate-function formula with Ψ(λ) = c2λ2 + O(λ3), yielding a qualitatively different threshold scaling β∗∼.

These K = 0 results serve as the “computation engine” for the companion paper (Nashida, 2026b), which proves that the rate function, critical threshold, and window-collapse asymptotics are K-independent for all K = o(d) via Dirichlet form comparison—with full O(log d) precision for K = O(1)—thereby extending the present results to the full NK landscape family.

Keywords: metastability, Metropolis dynamics, hypercube, birth-death chain, capacity theory, Laplace asymptotics, NK landscape, fitness landscape

1 Introduction

1.1 Background

Consider a population evolving on the binary hypercube {0,1}d under single-bit Metropolis dynamics driven by a fitness function. This is a natural discrete model of evolutionary search on a rugged fitness landscape: a single mutation is proposed uniformly at random and accepted or rejected according to the Metropolis rule weighted by a selection coefficient s > 0. When the fitness function decomposes additively as in the NK model of Kauffman and Levin (1987); Kauffman (1993), the parameter K (the number of epistatic interactions per locus) controls the ruggedness of the landscape. The extreme case K = 0 produces a smooth landscape in which the fitness depends only on the Hamming weight h(x) = ∑ ixi, and the Metropolis chain projects exactly onto a birth-death chain on {0,…,d}.

This projection is the starting point of the present paper. On the projected birth-death chain, classical tools from one-dimensional potential theory—the series resistance formula for capacity, the Karlin–McGregor hitting-time formula, and discrete Laplace asymptotics—become available with full rigour. We exploit these tools to establish three exact exponential-scale results: equilibrium delocalization for subextensive selection, a rate-function formula log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ(λ) + O(log d) for the metastable crossing time, and sharp threshold asymptotics with vanishing intermediate window.

1.2 Prior work

BEGK capacity theory. The potential-theoretic approach to metastability, developed by Bovier, Eckhoff, Gayrard, and Klein (Bovier et al., 2001, 2002) and expounded in Bovier and den Hollander (2015), expresses metastable transition times in terms of capacity (equivalently, effective resistance) and equilibrium measure. For continuous-state diffusions, this yields Eyring–Kramers-type prefactors. In our discrete setting, the birth-death projection reduces the capacity to a single sum (the effective resistance of a series circuit), making the Laplace asymptotics entirely explicit.

Curie–Weiss mixing times. Ding et al. (2009) established sharp mixing-time asymptotics for the mean-field Ising (Curie–Weiss) model, obtaining a mixing window of width O() near the critical inverse temperature. The present work obtains a qualitatively different window width: O((log d)∕d) in Regime I (interior saddle) and O((log T + log d)∕) in Regime II (boundary saddle, reflecting the quadratic vanishing of Ψ). The distinction arises because our rate function Ψ vanishes linearly in Regime I but quadratically in Regime II, rather than from a difference in the underlying Laplace methodology.

NK landscapes. The NK model was introduced by Kauffman and Levin (1987) as a tunably rugged fitness landscape for evolutionary biology. Simulation studies (Nashida, 2026c) reveal a three-phase structure—diffusive, punctuated, and frozen—with regime boundaries that are empirically K-independent for K ≪d. The present paper provides the analytical foundations for this observation by computing the exact rate function at K = 0.

1.3 Contribution and structure

This paper establishes three theorems for the K = 0 single-bit Metropolis chain on {0,1}d.

(i)
Equilibrium delocalization (Theorem 2.2 ): when β = o(d), the stationary mass in the endpoint basins vanishes. This is an unconditional result requiring only smoothness and symmetry of the basin function B.
(ii)
Regime I metastable asymptotics (Theorem 2.4 ): for smooth B admitting a supercritical double-well landscape with an interior saddle at msad ∈ (h∗,1∕2) and critical threshold λc > 0, the mean crossing time satisfies log 𝔼μL d[τRd] = dΨ(λ) + O(log d) with Ψ′(λc) > 0, yielding freezing-threshold asymptotics λfr = λc + log T∕(dΨ′(λc)) + O((log T)2∕d2 + (log d)∕d).
(iii)
Regime II metastable asymptotics (Theorem 2.5 ): for piecewise-smooth B with a kink at m = 1∕2 (no interior saddle; barrier minimum at the boundary), the rate function satisfies Ψ(λ) = c2λ2 + O(λ3) with c 2 > 0, yielding the qualitatively different threshold β∗ ∼.

Companion papers. This is the first paper in a series of two. The companion paper (Nashida, 2026b) proves that the rate function Ψ, the critical threshold λc, and the derivative Ψ′(λc) are K-independent for K = o(d), using Dirichlet form comparison to reduce the K > 0 case to K = 0. Combined with the present results, this yields log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ(λ) + O(log d) for K = O(1), with gracefully degrading error for growing K (Nashida, 2026b, Remark 2.3). The simulation study (Nashida, 2026c) provides the numerical discovery of the three-phase structure and its K-independence.

Section 4 proves Theorem 2.4 (Regime I). Section 5 proves Theorem 2.5 (Regime II). Section 6 discusses connections and open problems. Appendices collect external results, the dependency graph, and a note on the max-form basin function.

6 Discussion

6.1 Comparison with Curie–Weiss mixing times

The mean-field Ising (Curie–Weiss) model on {0,1}d shares the key structural feature of a Hamming-weight projection yielding a birth-death chain with a double-well free energy. Ding et al. (2009) established a mixing-time cutoff at βc = 1 with window width O() in the inverse-temperature parameter. In our framework, the Regime I window width is O((log d)∕d)—the same order in d when log T = Θ(log d). The difference is that our results concern the hitting time between basins (a metastability observable) rather than the mixing time (a global equilibration observable), and hold for general basin functions satisfying (A1)–(A5).

Regime II has no direct Curie–Weiss analogue, since the quadratic vanishing Ψ(λ) ∼c2λ2 and the scaling of β∗ arise from the boundary saddle—a feature absent in smooth models. More broadly, Regime II represents a structurally distinct metastable universality pattern: the boundary saddle produces a qualitatively different barrier onset law (quadratic rather than linear), a different threshold scaling (β∗ ∼ rather than β∗ ∼d), and a different window width (O((log T + log d)∕) rather than O((log d)∕d)). This distinction is not an artifact of our assumptions but reflects genuinely different free-energy geometries: a smooth interior saddle that “opens up” linearly versus a kink that forces quadratic onset.

6.2 Extension to K > 0

The companion paper (Nashida, 2026b) proves that for K = o(d) (intensive normalization κ = κ0∕d), the Dirichlet form of the K > 0 chain and the K = 0 birth-death chain are comparable. This K-independence result has a precise mechanism: the NK perturbation affects conductances by at most eO(κ0) = O(1) per edge, which is absorbed into the O(log d) error in the Laplace asymptotics. The precision of the final result depends on the growth rate of K: for K = O(1), the full O(log d) precision is achieved, yielding log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ(λ) + O(log d); for growing K, the error degrades to O(log d + sκ0(K+1)). In all cases, the rate function Ψ, critical value λc, and derivative Ψ′(λc) computed here remain the leading-order quantities; see (Nashida, 2026b, Remark 2.3) for the precise error hierarchy.

6.3 Open problems

(1)
Sharp Eyring–Kramers prefactor. The present results give log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ + O(log d). A natural refinement would be log 𝔼[τ] = dΨ + log d + C + o(1), where the log d arises from the Gaussian prefactor and C involves Φλ′′(m+), Φλ′′(msad), and pk∗+.
(2)
Subcritical hitting time. For λ < λc in Regime I, 𝔼μL d[τRd] is also exponentially large (the single-well barrier), but the fine structure is not addressed here.
(3)
Extensive NK normalization. When κ = O(1) (extensive), the perturbation per edge is O(d), and the K = 0 projection is no longer the correct leading-order description.
(4)
Formal verification. The proofs are structured to be amenable to formalization in Lean 4 or Coq, with the main obstacles being current gaps in Mathlib’s finite Markov chain infrastructure.

C On the max-form basin function

The basin function B(m) = max(e−2m2,e−2(1−m)2) fails to be C1 at m = 1∕2, so it falls outside assumption (A1) of Theorem 2.4. The following alternatives are available.

(a) Smooth substitute. Bγ(m) = γ−1 log(exp(γe−2m2) + exp(γe−2(1−m)2)) is C∞ and converges uniformly to the max-type B as γ →∞. Theorem 2.4 applies to this family (subject to verification of (A3)–(A5)).

(b) Sum substitute. B(m) = e−2m2 + e−2(1−m)2 is C∞ with the same qualitative basin structure. Theorem 2.4 applies (subject to verification of (A3)–(A5), confirmed numerically).

(c) Piecewise treatment. Theorem 2.5 covers the max-form B directly, yielding the explicit asymptotics in Corollary 2.6.
